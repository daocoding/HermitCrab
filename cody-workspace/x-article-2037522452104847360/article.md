Why Biology Is the Hardest Science

[Derya Unutmaz, MD](https://x.com/DeryaTR_)

[@DeryaTR_](https://x.com/DeryaTR_)

[18K](https://x.com/DeryaTR_/status/2037525393637335473/analytics)

Biology is often experienced as “the hardest science” not because it is less rigorous than physics, but because it is harder to compress into a small set of universally predictive, easily testable laws—especially when you care about real organisms in real environments. Living systems are deeply multiscale (molecules → cells → tissues → organs → organisms → ecosystems), and the interactions across these layers are not just additive. They generate emergent properties, system behaviors that cannot be straightforwardly read off from any one part in isolation. Modern systems biology makes this point explicitly: to understand biological function, we often must study structure + dynamics of networks, not isolated components, because system-level properties like robustness emerge from interactions. [[1]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11872829/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

Biology is uniquely difficult because the objects it studies are adaptive, historically contingent products of evolution, and noisy at their core. Organisms are not passive matter obeying fixed equations; they are evolved control systems that sense, learn, buffer perturbations, and reconfigure themselves across timescales. “Plasticity” (the ability of one genotype to produce different phenotypes across environments) and “canalization” (buffering that stabilizes development against perturbations) exemplify the fact that biological causality is often conditional and state-dependent, rather than universal and context-free. [[2]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4815458/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

Disease makes these difficulties concrete. Even for a familiar condition like type 2 diabetes, causal influence propagates across scales: molecular insulin signaling, cellular stress and β-cell dysfunction, tissue inflammation, organ-level glucose fluxes, behavior (diet, sleep, activity), and environment (food availability, stressors, pathogens, pollutants) all interact. Authoritative clinical syntheses describe type 2 diabetes as involving progressive β-cell dysfunction on a background of insulin resistance and broader multi-organ dysregulation, an inherently multiscale picture. [[3]](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes)

## What “hardest science” means in practice

Calling a science “hard” can mean several distinct things, each tied to what scientists and society want from explanations. A field is “easier” when it can (a) reduce many phenomena to a small set of stable principles, (b) make precise quantitative predictions that remain accurate across contexts, and (c) run controlled experiments with high measurement precision and repeatability. The The Feynman Lectures on Physics[[4]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11872829/?utm_source=chatgpt.com), associated with Richard Feynman[[5]](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/what-makes-biology-unique/5B4A83616082B6A6AB7DC13C33750AA1),captures a classic physics ideal: physics seeks compact “laws” that summarize enormous ranges of observations and are continually refined via experiment, with errors often framed in terms of measurement accuracy and approximation regimes. [[6]](https://www.feynmanlectures.caltech.edu/I_01.html)

Biology, by contrast, is often harder along exactly these axes, not because it lacks laws or mathematics, but because (i) the relevant “laws” are frequently conditional (“in this cell type, under this nutrient state, in this developmental window…”), (ii) the systems actively change the rules by adapting, and (iii) the space of possible mechanisms is combinatorially vast (many interacting parts, many potential feedback loops, many redundant pathways). Ernst Mayr[[7]](https://www.nature.com/articles/238413a0?utm_source=chatgpt.com) argued in What Makes Biology Unique?[[8]](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/what-makes-biology-unique/5B4A83616082B6A6AB7DC13C33750AA1) that biology has a distinctive autonomy as a science partly because evolutionary history and organismal organization introduce explanatory modes that are not reducible to physics-style laws alone. [[9]](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/what-makes-biology-unique/5B4A83616082B6A6AB7DC13C33750AA1)

A useful way to interpret “biology is hardest” is therefore: biology has a comparatively low “compression ratio.” You can compress classical gravity to a compact mathematical form and deploy it across planets and pendulums (within domains where the approximation holds), but you cannot compress “metabolic disease” or “immune dysregulation” into a similarly small set of universally accurate equations without losing essential context. This is one reason systems biology and network medicine emphasize interacting networks and modules rather than single-factor causes: disease phenotypes rarely emerge from one abnormal gene product; they reflect network-level disruptions shaped by context. [[10]](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrg2918?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

This comparison reflects general characterizations of physical law as compact summaries tested and refined by experiment, and of biological explanation as frequently networked, multiscale, and constrained by measurement and intervention limits. (7)

## Multiscale layering and emergent properties

Biology is not one system but a stack of interacting systems. Molecules form networks inside cells; cells communicate and mechanically couple inside tissues; tissues coordinate within organs; organs coordinate within organisms; organisms reshape and respond to ecosystems. Each step introduces new effective variables, new constraints, and new feedback loops. A key practical point from systems biology is that system-level properties (robustness, homeostatic regulation, failure modes) cannot be reliably inferred by studying parts in isolation, because interactions reshape behavior. [[1]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11872829/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

A rigorous way to say “emergence” is: the mapping from microstate to macro-behavior is many-to-one, nonlinear, and interaction-dependent. “Emergent properties” are not mystical; they are what you get when many components interact so that system behavior depends on network topology, feedback, and coupling strengths. Two canonical empirical/theoretical anchors illustrate this. First, in development and pattern formation, Alan Turing[[12]](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrg1471?utm_source=chatgpt.com) showed that reaction–diffusion dynamics can generate stable spatial patterns (“Turing patterns”) from homogeneous starting conditions—an early, quantitative theory of how macroscopic biological form can arise from local interactions. [[13]](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rstb/article/237/641/37/112910/The-chemical-basis-of-morphogenesis?utm_source=chatgpt.com) Second, in ecology, classical work on stability in large complex systems established that adding more interacting components can qualitatively change system stability regimes—showing that “more parts” can mean “new behavior,” not merely “scaled-up behavior.” [[14]](https://www.nature.com/articles/238413a0?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

Multiscale coupling makes causality “long-range” in biology: a molecular change can alter organ function; organ dysfunction can feed back as hormonal changes that alter gene expression; behavior can reshape physiology; environments can change selective pressures or exposures that alter molecular damage rates. Network medicine formalizes the clinical version of this statement by emphasizing that disease phenotypes reflect interacting pathobiological processes in networks rather than single linear chains. [[15]](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrg2918?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

## Adaptation, plasticity, and historical contingency

Physics confronts complexity, but many physical objects do not adapt in the active sense: they do not sense, learn, rewire, or evolve new functional architectures on experimental timescales. Biology does. Adaptation occurs developmentally (cells differentiating), physiologically (metabolic and immune adjustments), behaviorally (learning), and evolutionarily (selection reshaping populations). This matters because it means biological “laws” are often conditional on internal state, history, and feedback control. Systems that change their own effective parameters are harder to predict than systems whose parameters are fixed. [[16]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12610534/)

Plasticity is especially destabilizing for simple prediction. At minimum, it means the same genotype can produce different phenotypes under different environments; more broadly, it means the same organism can express different functional regimes depending on context and past exposures. Reviews in evolutionary developmental biology (“evo-devo”) emphasize that plasticity is not a peripheral curiosity; it is a central feature linking development, ecology, and evolution. [[17]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4815458/?utm_source=chatgpt.com) In molecular terms, epigenetic mechanisms provide one route by which environmental signals can alter stable gene expression states without changing DNA sequence, giving biology a memory-like capacity across cell generations. [[18]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12610534/)

Evolution adds another difficulty: history matters, and history cannot be rerun. Biological systems are “path dependent”—their present possibilities are constrained by the specific sequence of past changes. Experimental evolution provides unusually clean evidence. In a long-term evolution experiment, a key innovation (a new capacity to exploit citrate under oxic conditions) appeared only after tens of thousands of generations and depended on prior “potentiating” mutations—direct evidence that adaptive outcomes can be contingent on historical paths rather than inevitable endpoints. [[19]](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0803151105?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

Historical contingency is also visible across deep time. Major transitions in Earth and life history, such as the Great Oxidation Event (a geochemical and biological transformation around ~2.4 billion years ago) and the origin/diversification of mitochondria via endosymbiosis—changed what kinds of organisms and metabolisms were even possible, reshaping the “search space” of evolutionary innovation. [[20]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0960982209011890) At smaller scales, striking innovations like antifreeze glycoproteins in Antarctic notothenioid fish have been traced to specific molecular origins (derivation from a trypsinogen gene), a reminder that even “obvious” adaptations can hinge on particular historical genetic raw materials. [[21]](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.94.8.3811)

Suggested mermaid diagram: a timeline that mixes deep-time and laboratory-time contingency examples (dates approximate; the point is path dependence, not exact chronology). [[22]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0960982209011890)

## Noise, context dependence, redundancy, and robustness

Even if evolution froze tomorrow, biology would still be difficult because living cells are noisy and heterogeneous. At the molecular scale, gene expression involves small numbers of molecules and stochastic reaction events. Experiments using fluorescent reporters in bacteria showed that even genetically identical cells can exhibit substantial cell-to-cell variability (“noise”) and distinguished intrinsic from extrinsic sources of gene expression noise. [[23]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12183631/?utm_source=chatgpt.com) Reviews and theoretical work generalize this: gene expression randomness appears across organisms and interacts with network structure, making variability sometimes beneficial (bet-hedging) and sometimes harmful (loss of control). [[24]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0092867408012439?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

Biological redundancy and robustness complicate inference because they hide causality. When systems buffer perturbations, “nothing happens” after you perturb one component—even though that component truly matters—because parallel pathways compensate. Large-scale gene deletion work in yeast demonstrated the scope of genome-wide functional redundancy and context dependence by systematically evaluating phenotypes across many deletions and conditions. [[25]](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature00935?utm_source=chatgpt.com) The robustness concept is not hand-waving: authoritative syntheses describe robustness as a ubiquitous property of biological systems, attained by architectural principles (feedback, modularity, redundancy) and intertwined with evolvability. [[26]](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrg1471?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

Context dependence, “the same perturbation means different things in different contexts”—is one of biology’s most operationally painful features. A clear example comes from targeted cancer therapy: inhibition of the oncogenic BRAF(V600E) pathway is not uniformly effective across tissues with the same mutation. In BRAF-mutant colon cancers, BRAF inhibition can trigger rapid feedback activation of EGFR that sustains proliferation, so combination strategies targeting EGFR can be required, demonstrating that network wiring differs by tissue context and changes therapeutic causality. [[27]](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature10868)

Even when investigators attempt strict standardization, biological outcomes can drift across environments. A classic multi-laboratory study of mouse behavior found substantial effects of laboratory environment despite rigorously equated apparatus and protocols—showing that hidden environmental variables and organismal plasticity can defeat naive assumptions of perfect reproducibility. [[28]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10356397/) Measurement pipelines themselves add context dependence: in high-throughput “omics,” batch effects (differences due to reagent lots, personnel, instruments, or run dates) can induce spurious signals unless explicitly designed for and corrected. [[29]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20838408/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

## Disease as a running case study across scales

Type 2 diabetes is a useful “stress test” for scientific explanation because it is neither purely genetic nor purely environmental, neither purely molecular nor purely behavioral. It is a multiscale syndrome in which molecular signaling, cellular health, tissue inflammation, organ cross-talk, and lived environment form a coupled system. The American Diabetes Association[[30]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12610534/) defines type 2 diabetes in its Standards of Care as involving progressive loss of adequate β-cell insulin secretion frequently on the background of insulin resistance, emphasizing heterogeneity and multi-pathway decline rather than a single lesion. [[31]](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes)

At the molecular layer, insulin action is mediated by signaling pathways that regulate glucose uptake and metabolism across tissues; canonical reviews detail how insulin signaling integrates carbohydrate and lipid metabolism and coordinates tissue cross-talk (liver, muscle, adipose) via signaling and transcriptional programs. [[32]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11742412/) At the cellular and tissue layers, obesity-associated inflammation links immune sensing with metabolic regulation: macrophage accumulation in adipose tissue and activation of inflammatory pathways provide one experimentally grounded mechanism by which tissue context shifts systemic insulin sensitivity. [[33]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14679176/)

At the organ and whole-body layers, a major clinical synthesis (the “ominous octet” framework) argues that type 2 diabetes reflects dysfunction across multiple organs and regulatory loops (β-cells, liver, muscle, adipose tissue, gut incretin system, kidney, brain), underscoring why single-target interventions often underperform and why combination strategies are common. [[34]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19336687/) Meanwhile, environmental and lifestyle factors are not “mere confounders” but active causal inputs. For example, the World Health Organization[[35]](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature00935?utm_source=chatgpt.com) highlights modifiable prevention levers, diet, physical activity, body weight, tobacco, indicating that population-level disease incidence is strongly shaped by environment and behavior alongside biology. [[36]](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/diabetes)

Even the microbial ecosystem layer can enter the causal loop. Metagenome-wide association studies have reported compositional and functional differences in gut microbiota associated with type 2 diabetes (“dysbiosis” patterns), suggesting additional cross-scale pathways (diet → microbiome → metabolites/inflammation → insulin sensitivity), though causality and directionality remain active research areas. [[37]](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature11450) The key point is not that “everything causes diabetes” (an unhelpful statement), but that plausible causal pathways exist at multiple, interacting layers—and interventions at one layer can be amplified, buffered, or redirected by dynamics at other layers. [[38]](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrg2918?utm_source=chatgpt.com)[[39]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19336687/)

## Implications for research methods, modeling, reproducibility, and education

Because biology is multiscale, adaptive, noisy, historically contingent, and measurement-constrained, research methods must be engineered around these realities rather than treated as inconveniences.

First, experiments must anticipate hidden heterogeneity and context. Biology’s intrinsic stochasticity and cell-to-cell variability mean that population averages can hide important substructures (rare states, divergent trajectories), motivating single-cell measurement approaches. Reviews of single-cell RNA sequencing emphasize both its promise (resolving tissue composition and transcriptional dynamics) and its fragility (complex workflows, quality control, batch effects, and inference challenges). [[40]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1097276515002610) Yet biological measurement often perturbs or destroys what it measures (for example, many sequencing workflows require cell isolation and molecular processing), which is fundamentally unlike many physics measurements where repeated non-destructive measurement is routine. This creates a structural obstacle to “perfect” experimentation in living systems. [[41]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1097276515002610)

Second, modeling in biology must often be pluralistic. Three broad model families matter:

Mechanistic models (e.g., differential equation models of signaling or physiology) aim for causal explanation but can be underdetermined because many parameters are hard to measure and multiple parameter sets can fit the same data. The “sloppy models” result in systems biology showed that many models have broad parameter directions that barely affect predictions, implying that even extensive data may not uniquely identify mechanisms unless experiments are designed for identifiability. [[42]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pcbi.0030189&utm_source=chatgpt.com) Statistical models (e.g., regression, hierarchical Bayesian models, causal inference) are often better matched to heterogeneity, uncertainty, and observational constraints, but they can struggle to extrapolate under interventions without causal structure. Hybrid models (combining mechanistic structure with statistical learning) are increasingly attractive because they can embed biological constraints while learning from rich data, but they inherit the difficulties of both worlds (assumption misspecification + dataset shift). [[43]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pcbi.0030189&utm_source=chatgpt.com)

Third, reproducibility in biology cannot be treated as a purely cultural failure; it is also a technical and epistemic challenge shaped by biological variability. Surveys and meta-discussions in the life sciences document widespread concern and debate: some authors emphasize failures to replicate and the need for better standards (especially in preclinical translation), while others warn that an overly simplistic “replication drive” can punish careful science when contexts legitimately differ and when direct replication is not always the right test. [[44]](https://www.nature.com/articles/483531a?utm_source=chatgpt.com) Official initiatives reflect this dual reality. The National Institutes of Health[[45]](https://www.hhs.gov/ohrp/regulations-and-policy/belmont-report/index.html) explicitly requires applicants to address rigor, transparency, relevant biological variables, and authentication of key resources, acknowledging that biomedical reproducibility depends on design, reporting, and materials control. [[46]](https://grants.nih.gov/policy-and-compliance/policy-topics/reproducibility/guidance) The National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine[[47]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20838408/?utm_source=chatgpt.com) similarly frames reproducibility and replicability as systemic issues with actionable recommendations across researchers, journals, and funders. [[48]](https://www.nationalacademies.org/our-work/reproducibility-and-replicability-in-science)

Fourth, measurement and experimentation are ethically constrained in biology in ways that have no close analog in many physics subfields. Human research is bounded by principled ethical frameworks—for example, the Belmont Report’s emphasis on respect for persons, beneficence, and justice—which restricts what interventions and randomizations are permissible. [[49]](https://www.hhs.gov/ohrp/regulations-and-policy/belmont-report/index.html) Modern causal claims in medicine therefore often rely on a patchwork: randomized trials where feasible (with reporting standards like CONSORT), natural experiments, mechanistic plausibility, and convergent triangulation across model systems and datasets. [[50]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3043330/)

These constraints also shape education. Physics education often orients around deriving and applying compact equations; by contrast, biology education must train systems thinking: comfort with layered explanations, probabilistic reasoning, feedback control, and context-sensitive causality. A practical educational implication is that students need earlier and more sustained exposure to (i) statistics and uncertainty, (ii) dynamical systems and networks, and (iii) experimental design under confounding and heterogeneity, because these are not “advanced topics” in biology—they are the daily substrate of interpretation. [[51]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20838408/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

Finally, “biology is hard” does not mean “physics is easy.” Physics contains deep hardness of its own, especially in nonlinear dynamics and fluids. The Clay Mathematics Institute[[52]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19336687/) characterizes foundational open questions about Navier–Stokes solutions (existence and smoothness), reflecting how even equations that “govern fluid flow” can resist full mathematical understanding, and chaos theory shows how deterministic systems (e.g., Lorenz’s system) can be unpredictably sensitive to initial conditions. [[53]](https://www.claymath.org/millennium/navier-stokes-equation/?utm_source=chatgpt.com) The distinctive claim about biology is therefore narrower: living systems combine nonlinear complexity with adaptation, historical contingency, and ethical/experimental constraints, producing a uniquely high barrier to universal, context-free predictability. [[54]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18524956/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

Major open questions and practical recommendations

A central open scientific question is how to build reliable bridges between genotype and phenotype across contexts. Modern genetics increasingly portrays complex traits as highly polygenic (many variants of small effect) with effects that can vary across populations and contexts, motivating frameworks like the “omnigenic” view and continued work on mapping variants to biological function. [[55]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28622505/) Another open question is how to construct multiscale models that are both predictive under intervention and interpretable enough to guide mechanism and therapy, given parameter sloppiness, heterogeneity, and context dependence. [[56]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pcbi.0030189&utm_source=chatgpt.com)

For researchers, a practical recommendation is to treat context as a first-class experimental variable rather than “noise.” Concretely: design perturbation experiments that deliberately span relevant contexts (cell types, developmental stages, metabolic states), measure key covariates that could drive heterogeneity, and pre-specify how you will distinguish intrinsic biological variability from technical artifacts like batch effects. [[57]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20838408/?utm_source=chatgpt.com) Another recommendation is to align model choice with scientific intent: use mechanistic models when you need intervention reasoning and can design identifiability-rich experiments; use statistical/hierarchical models when heterogeneity dominates; and use hybrid approaches only when you can justify constraints and validate out-of-distribution performance. [[58]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pcbi.0030189&utm_source=chatgpt.com)
